# Behavioral

The half of interviews that everyone under-prepares for.

## STAR

Every story fits the same shape:

- **Situation** — one sentence of context.
- **Task** — what you needed to accomplish, and the constraint.
- **Action** — what YOU did specifically (not "we").
- **Result** — the measurable outcome. If negative, what you learned.

Two minutes max per story. Practice out loud.

## Stories to have ready

Draft one story for each. If you can't fill a bucket, that's a signal — go
build the experience or think harder about your past work.

1. **A time you shipped under an unrealistic deadline.**
2. **A time you owned a production incident.**
3. **A time you disagreed with a senior engineer and how you handled it.**
4. **A technical decision you'd make differently now.**
5. **A time you improved a slow system (with numbers).**
6. **A time you mentored a peer.**
7. **A time you asked for help.**
8. **A time you rewrote/deprecated your own code.**
9. **A time you made your team's day-to-day faster.**
10. **A time you delivered feedback that changed someone's behavior.**

## Common mistakes

- "We did X" — the interviewer wants **your** contribution.
- Long, meandering setup, thin action, no result.
- Choosing a story that flatters you but doesn't answer the question.
- No numbers on the result — "much faster" is not a metric.

## Questions to ask the interviewer

Prepare 5–7. Signals interest and gives you data.

- What does a great engineer look like six months in?
- Walk me through the last hard bug your team shipped a fix for.
- How is technical work planned — top-down, bottom-up, mixed?
- How does the team handle disagreement?
- What surprised you when you joined?

## Senior-level nuances

- Have a "hard call" story. Not "when did you succeed" but "when did you
  choose the less good option because of constraints." Adults live here.
- Own the failures. Explain what changed as a result.
